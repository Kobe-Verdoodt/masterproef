A-01 Redundancy
Evaluates the extent to which information is unnecessarily repeated in the text.
1: Extreme redundancy; multiple paragraphs or sentences convey the exact same data without adding value
3: Some filler content present; occasional restatement of obvious facts that could be streamlined
5: Zero waste; every sentence provides new, unique information essential to the reader's understanding

A-02 Repetition
Focuses on the repeated use of words or phrases that may not contribute to redundancy but can impact the flow and reader engagement
1: Highly repetitive vocabulary; creates a monotonous and unprofessional tone
3: Noticeable repetition of key terms; the flow is slightly disrupted but the meaning remains clear
5: Excellent lexical diversity; synonyms and varied sentence structures keep the reader engaged and the prose fluid

B-01 Ambiguity due to Lack of Detail
Assesses instances in which insufficient details are provided.
1: Vague claims without any supporting evidence
3: General details provided, but leaves the reader with "how" or "what" questions regarding implementation
5: Granular detail; provides specific actions, methods, and parameters

B-02 Ambiguity in Causation
Determines how causes and effects are described in the text
1: Correlational claims presented as causation with no logical link; confusing "if-then" structures
3: Causal links are present but weak or require the reader to make assumptions
5: Explicit and verifiable causal chains; clearly explains how Input A leads to Output B

B-03 Ambiguity in Modifiers
Concerns the use of adjectives, adverbs, and other modifiers that are vague or poorly placed.
1: Overuse of subjective "puffery" (e.g., "extremely good," "very significant") without definition
3: Occasional use of imprecise modifiers like "most," "many," or "often" where specifics were possible
5: Modifiers are precise and restricted to objective descriptors

C-01 Quantitative Ambiguity
Pertains to the unclear use of material numbers, statistics, or quantities that carry significant weight or meaning, while excluding incidental data such as dates or non-essential figures.
1: Numbers are provided without units, scale, or meaning
3: Data is provided with context, but the calculation or source is slightly opaque
5: Absolute precision; numbers are tied to specific formulas and benchmarks

C-02 Temporal Ambiguity
Involves unclear or vague references to time
1: Vague terms like "soon," "recently," or "periodically" with no timeframe
3: Broad timeframes given (e.g., "annually") but specific dates or triggers for updates are missing
5: Fixed temporal markers; specific frequencies and historical ranges are defined

C-03 Percentage Ambiguity
Addresses the use of percentages or proportional data given without adequate explanation or baseline
1: E.g. "Increase of 20%" with no indication of the starting value or the total population.
3: Percentages are used, but the "denominator" is only implied, not stated
5: Full transparency; percentages are clearly linked to a defined total 

C-04 Scope Ambiguity
Deals with the uncertainty over the extent or range of a claim or statement, such as which parts of an organization or which timescales are being referred to
1: Unclear if a statement applies to a single company, an industry, or the global universe
3: Scope is generally defined but has "gray areas"
5: Clearly demarcated boundaries

C-05 Contextual Ambiguity
Determines the overall lack of context that can leave readers unsure about the relevance or significance of the information provided
1: Raw data dumped without explaining why it matters
3: Information is relevant, but its specific weight in the "big picture" is not immediately clear
5: Deep context provided; every metric is placed within a hierarchy 

C-06 Interpretative Ambiguity
Focuses on text that can be interpreted in multiple ways due to vague or nonspecific language
1: Language is so vague it could be used by a company to "greenwash" or hide underperformance
3: Mostly clear, but certain terms could be misunderstood by a non-expert
5: "Single-meaning" prose; technical definitions ensure there is only one way to interpret the data

D. External Reliance
D-01 Context Dependency
Evaluates how well the text stands alone without needing additional information or external references to be fully understood
1: Constant "see website" or "refer to external document" for core logic; the text is a shell
3: Major logic is present, but understanding specific examples requires looking at external appendices
5: Fully self-contained; the methodology, examples, and definitions are all provided within the document
